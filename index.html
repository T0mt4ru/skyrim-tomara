Tomara: The Pure Redguard

"No Second Chances" Legendary Difficulty Playthrough

Character Overview

  • Name: Tomara
  • Race: Redguard (Female)
  • Game: The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim
  • Difficulty: Legendary
  • Core Mod: "No Second Chances".
  • Civil War Faction: Stormcloaks (chosen during Helgen escape due to anti-Imperial sentiment).
  • Current Level: 4

Perks

  • Level 3: Armsman (1/5): One-Handed weapons do 20% more damage.
  • Level 4: Novice Alteration: Cast Novice level Alteration spells for half magicka.

Tomara's Tale

Chapter 1: The Executioner's Shadow

The jarring rumble of the cart was the only constant. My hands, bound by coarse rope, chafed against each other. My head throbbed, a dull ache behind my eyes, and my stomach churned with a mixture of fear and confusion. I remembered a sudden ambush, the clash of steel, and then… darkness. Now, this, a prisoner cart, rattling me towards an unknown fate.

"Wake up, Redguard," a gruff voice beside me mumbled. It was one of the men in Stormcloak furs. He looked as weary and beaten as I felt. "We're almost there. Helgen."

Helgen. The name echoed ominously. The chill in the air bit harder as we passed under a stone archway. Imperial soldiers lined the road, their faces grim, their spears pointed. This was no ordinary prison. This was... an execution. My heart hammered against my ribs. I saw the block, the axe, the grim-faced executioner. My breath hitched. This was it.

The captain, a stern woman with an unyielding gaze, read off names. One by one, the Nords, these 'Stormcloaks,' were called. Each step towards the block felt like my own. I wasn't a Stormcloak. I wasn't even from Skyrim. What business did I have dying here, at the hands of the Empire, for a war that wasn't mine? My fury simmered, hot and impotent.

Just as the man next to me, Ralof, was about to be called, a monstrous roar ripped through the air. The ground trembled. The sky darkened, not with clouds, but with something immense, something black and leathery. A shadow, larger than any bird, blotted out the midday sun, casting an unnatural chill over the stone walls of Helgen. Chaos erupted. Soldiers screamed, civilians scattered. My chance. My only chance.

Chapter 2: Dragon's Fury, Dungeon's Escape

The creature from the sky tore through Helgen, a terrifying, majestic force. In the chaos, Ralof, the Stormcloak from the cart, shouted for me to follow him. He led me through the burning streets, dodging falling debris and charging through what remained of the Imperial ranks. We plunged into the dungeons beneath the keep, a labyrinth of dark corridors and cramped cells.

The Imperials still hunted us here. We fought our way through them. Ralof with his axe, and I, having quickly snatched an Imperial sword from a fallen soldier, put it to good use. Each clash was swift and brutal; they fell quickly, as did any who managed to land a blow on us. We found a few Imperial bows and daggers among the fallen, crude but usable.

Deeper into the caves, the air grew thick with webbing. Giant spiders. Their skittering legs and venomous fangs were a new horror. I took up one of the Imperial bows and let fly, finding a surprising natural rhythm even with an unfamiliar weapon. My arrows thudded home, making short work of the arachnids, their chitinous bodies cracking under the force.

We reached the cavern exit, fresh air tantalizingly close. But a low growl rumbled from the shadows – a bear, massive and territorial. Ralof whispered, "Let's try to sneak past it, Redguard. No need for a fight."

I glanced at him. Sneaking? That wasn't my way. Never had been. I was a daughter of Hammerfell, direct and unyielding. I gripped the bow tighter, pulling an arrow from the quiver. "No," I stated, my voice firm. "We'll go through it."

I drew the string back, the Imperial bow surprisingly sturdy. The bear roared, lumbering forward. I held my breath, focused, and released. The arrow flew true, piercing its thick hide. The beast stumbled, a pained grunt escaping its maw, and then collapsed. It. It was over as quickly as it began. Ralof looked at me, a mixture of surprise and approval on his face. We had escaped.

Chapter 3: The Warrior's Blessing

With the bear dispatched, the path to freedom was clear. Ralof and I emerged from the cave, breathing in the crisp, cold air of Skyrim. We walked in comfortable silence for a time, heading south, the landscape around us a mixture of ancient forests and towering peaks. Along the way, Ralof pointed out the Guardian Stones. Three massive, weathered stones stood sentinel, glowing faintly with ancient magic. He explained they offered blessings.

I approached the Warrior Stone. Its rough surface felt cool beneath my fingertips. I didn't know what kind of blessing it truly offered, but I closed my eyes and focused my will, a silent prayer to whatever ancient spirits might be listening. *Grant me strength*, I thought, *sharpness for my blade, accuracy for my bow, and resilience for my flesh. Let my path be direct, my strikes true, and my enemies fall quickly.* For in this harsh land, with its unfamiliar customs and dangerous creatures, I would need every ounce of fortune I could find.

Soon, the smoke plumes of Riverwood appeared. A small logging town nestled by a river. "My sister, Gerdur, lives here," Ralof said, a hint of relief in his voice. "She can help us. She'll have supplies, and a roof over our heads for a while." Supplies. The word was a heavy thought. Everything I had, I'd taken from a corpse or found by chance. I would need to find more.

Chapter 4: A Sister's Shelter

Ralof led me to Gerdur's house in Riverwood. His sister, a woman with kind but wary eyes, was indeed guarded at first. I explained how I knew Ralof, how we'd met under the most dire of circumstances in Helgen. She listened intently, her skepticism evident, until I spoke of the creature, the one that had torn the town apart. The sheer impossibility of the tale, recounted with the raw terror of experience, seemed to convince her. The doubt in her eyes slowly gave way to a chilling realization.

She provided what supplies she could spare – a few potions, some bread, a leather cuirass and boots. Nothing I could trade, but vital for survival. She then urged me to go to Whiterun, the hold's capital, to inform the Jarl. "He needs to know," she insisted, her voice urgent. She pointed me towards the path leading north, a new, heavy destination now set before me.

Before setting off, I found a quiet spot near the river. The Imperial bow I'd scavenged from Helgen was crude, but it was mine. I ran my fingers along the wood, examining its balance, testing the string. It wasn't a fine Yokudan blade, but it was a tool, and a tool could always be improved. I worked on it, honing its shape, tightening the string, using what little knowledge of smithing I possessed from my youth. It felt better, more responsive, ready for the hunts to come.

Chapter 5: Whiterun's Welcome

The journey to Whiterun was long, and the city gates were shut tight by the time I arrived under the cloak of night. Guards, wary and grim, challenged me immediately, citing dragon attacks. "I'm a survivor of Helgen," I told them, my voice weary but firm. That seemed to shift something in their hardened expressions, and with a grudging nod, they let me in. Gerdur's instruction to inform the Jarl had felt strangely misinformed; they already knew.

I found my way to The Bannered Mare, a tavern bustling even at this late hour. My meager supply of gold was enough for a room, a temporary respite. As I settled in, a loud, unpleasant voice grated on my ears. A man, obviously the local bard, was pestering a woman. My Hammerfell blood boiled. I wouldn't stand by. With a swiftness born of my recent trials, I moved. A few well-placed strikes, a shove, and the bard, Mikael, found himself sprawling. The woman, clearly relieved, thanked me profusely, offering me a handful of fresh ingredients – vegetables, meat, enough to make a hearty soup. While I couldn't dabble in potion-making, a simple, fortifying meal was always welcome.

The next morning, refreshed, I made for Dragonsreach, the imposing castle atop the city. Barely through the entrance, I was intercepted by Irileth, the Jarl's stern-faced housecarl. Another explanation. Another recounting of fire and terror. Eventually, I was granted an audience with Jarl Balgruuf himself. He listened, his brow furrowed, confirming his own scouts had reported on the Helgen devastation.

He thanked me, his voice grave, and offered me a piece of steel armor. I took it, then promptly discarded it behind me as I turned. I wore no armor. My flesh was my shield, honed by speed and will, aided only by my Alteration spells. Balgruuf then suggested I assist his court wizard, Farengar Secret-Fire, with his dragon research.

Farengar. A sneering, arrogant man who clearly thought himself superior to all. He hardly looked up from his arcane texts as I approached. He spoke of ancient knowledge, of the dragon threat, and then, with a dismissive wave, gave me two tasks: deliver some frost salts to Arcadia, the local alchemist, and then venture into Bleak Falls Barrow, an old barrow near Riverwood, to retrieve a stone tablet. So, back to Riverwood I would go.

Chapter 6: The Golden Claw's Secret

The road from Riverwood to Bleak Falls Barrow was indeed guarded by bandits. Lookouts, perhaps, drawn by whispers of treasure within the ancient tomb. I moved with caution, picking off a few with well-aimed arrows to the head. The rest charged, but my Imperial sword met them swiftly, ending the confrontation before it truly began. Yet, the cold was worse than the bandits. My fingers grew numb, hindering my draw, and I knew I couldn't risk another arrow until the feeling returned.

I sought immediate refuge inside the barrow. More bandits awaited, but the warmth within was a welcome relief. My fingers thawed, allowing me to nock another arrow, felling one of them. The other fell to the keen edge of my blade, a slice that found its mark with deadly precision. They had set up a crude camp, complete with a fire. The crackling flames promised warmth and a moment of peace, so I rested there for about an hour, letting my body recover.

Pushing deeper, I found another bandit, utterly perplexed by an ancient locking mechanism. He must have been at it for hours, oblivious to my presence. I drew my bow, but before I could release the arrow, poisoned darts shot from hidden crevices, striking him down. These traps, I realized, were not to be underestimated. I solved the puzzle myself; the hints were obvious, carved onto the columns above.

Past some scurrying rodents, I heard frantic screams echoing through the chambers. Rounding a corner, I saw a Dunmer tangled in a colossal web. As I moved closer, the monstrous shadow of a giant Frostbite Spider loom. I recoiled, my blade flashing out as I unleashed a stream of fire. The combination of steel and flame brought the wounded beast down quickly. The Dunmer, Arvel the Swift, introduced himself before foolishly fleeing deeper into the barrow, unwilling to share his 'treasure.' Little did he know, my purpose wasn't gold.

I followed Arvel into the burial chambers. The dead here were not resting. Draugr, guardians beyond the grave, stirred from their slumber. Arvel, for all his 'swiftness,' offered little resistance before being cut down. I kept my distance, the rest from earlier having fully returned the feeling to my fingers. Most of them fell to my arrows. Taking precautions, I even took down some of the 'resting' Draugr with preemptive shots. A few still managed to close the distance, but my blade handled them. More traps lay ahead – swinging blades. These Nords certainly protected their dead with fervor.

I eventually reached a large door, adorned with peculiar discs and symbols. Symbols I recognized from the Golden Claw I'd taken from Arvel's pockets. Three symbols, three discs – it was a key. Using the claw, the ancient mechanism ground open, revealing yet another part of the barrow.

After dispatching more Draugr, I arrived in the main chamber. A strange wall hummed with an ethereal energy that captivated me. I was almost too enthralled to notice the formidable Draugr behind me stirring. This one was tougher, clearly a guardian of some importance, and it clutched a stone tablet. I unleashed fire spells and struck with my blade, finally felling it. The tablet, the Dragonstone, was now mine. Time to head back to Farengar.

I noticed an exit at the back of the main chamber, leading directly outside to Lake Ilinalta. Would have been useful to know about that entrance earlier. It wasn't far from Riverwood, and with evening approaching, I decided to rest there for the night before making the trek back to Whiterun. Extra Irileth guards had already set up camp just outside town, a clear sign of the growing unease. I also spotted some corundum and iron veins by the lake; plenty of ore to turn into steel once I reach a forge.

With a new steel sword taking shape under my hammer, the rhythmic clang against the anvil was a familiar comfort. No more Imperial blade, a lingering shadow of Helgen's horror. This new sword would be a testament to my own journey, my own strength. I honed its edge until it gleamed, sharp and ready. It was a clean start, a blade forged in my will, not in the Empire's grim purpose.

Chapter 9: The First Dragon Slain

We arrived at the Western Watchtower, its stone walls scarred by age. There was no dragon in sight, only the quiet, chilling aftermath of a recent attack. The guards marched in, swords drawn, their bravado a stark contrast to the true threat we faced. Fools, I thought. Did they truly believe a sharpened piece of steel on the ground could contend with a flying beast? I had seen the horror at Helgen. These things flew.

I gripped my bow, a more sensible weapon for a creature of the air. Better to shoot pointy sticks at it from a distance than to let it come to your pointy stick. Once inside the tower, a surviving guard, shaken but alive, warned us to stay back. All might seem clear, he insisted, but he was sure the beast was still around. His words were cut short by a roar, mighty and loud, that vibrated through the very stones of the tower.

The other guards, their earlier bravado shattered, went running out. But I held my ground. This beast flew, and I was going to get on equal footing this time. I made my way to the top of the tower, scanning the horizon.

And then I saw it. Rising from behind the mountains, a dark silhouette against the sky. In the blink of an eye, it had closed the distance, a terrifying blur of scales and wings. I carefully knocked my arrows, taking a deep breath, trying to calm the pounding in my chest. Last time was chaos; this time, we were prepared. My first arrow shot true, finding its mark in the creature's massive wing. I loosed a few more, my aim unerring, making riddled cheese out of its leathery membranes.

It came tumbling down, roaring, yelling as if speaking, a torrent of flames erupting from its muzzle. The guards, now emboldened, closed in, swords in hand, while I stayed atop the tower. I knew better than to approach a beast that wasn't slain yet. Even grounded, its power was immense. Shot after shot, I fired into the beast's skull, my arrows piercing its tough hide, while the guards bravely swung at its legs. And then, finally, the beast ceased to breathe. A secluded monastery where they studied the Thu'um, the dragon's voice. They believed I was the one they had been waiting for; the last to be summoned had been Tiber Septim himself.

These Nords and their ancient traditions. I, a daughter of Hammerfell, had no Atmora roots, no lineage to their mythical heroes. Yet, this entire experience felt... different. Strange, yes, but undeniably powerful. I had to admit, the call resonated deep within me. I decided to heed the summons.

Chapter 13: The Ascendant Path

Recovery took longer than expected. By the time I felt ready to make the climb to High Hrothgar, night had fallen. Evidentially, it's high up in the mountains, and I recalled the biting cold from Bleak Falls Barrow, the numbness in my fingers. There was no way I was going to make this treacherous climb in the dead and cold embrace of the night.

Instead, I sought out Klimmek, the man the priestess of Mara had suggested. It turned out he regularly climbed the steps to bring supplies to the Greybeards, but had recently twisted his ankle. I offered to take the supplies with me, since I was heading up there anyway. True to Mara's earlier suggestion, I also assisted him with his "love triangle" predicament, ultimately swaying events in his favor with Bassianus and Fastred. Perhaps Mara wasn't so useless after all, if her bidding indirectly led to a warm bed and a clear conscience.

Morning arrived, crisp and cold, and I began my ascent up the 7000 steps. The journey was a pilgrimage, dotted with shrines every so often – ten in total. I made sure to visit each one, a brief moment of quiet contemplation before continuing. The trip was broken up by other random pilgrims, persistent packs of wolves, and even curious herds of goats. The air grew progressively colder the higher I climbed, the numbing chill starting to creep into my fingers despite the exertion.

Near the end, with the distant outline of the monastery finally in sight, I spotted a frost troll. Its hulking form was unmistakable. I doused an arrow in some frostbite venom and nocked it, holding my breath, the sheer cold already numbing my fingers. But the troll remained oblivious to my presence. Carefully, I unknocked the poisonous arrow. No need to waste valuable venom just to fell a troll if I could just pass by unnoticed.

A few more steps, and two final shrines later, I felt a profound sense of calm wash over me. The air was still cold, biting even, but somehow, being so high up, disconnected from the squabbles of the lower lands, had a strangely calming effect. I reached the large wooden door of the monastery, Klimmek's supplies tucked securely under my arm, and knocked.
